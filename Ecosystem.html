Ecosystem Services

Mangrove forests, often overlooked treasures along coastlines, provide an array of crucial ecosystem services that contribute to the well-being of local and regional communities.

Ecosystem services, defined as the welfare contributions and benefits people derive from ecosystems, are aptly labeled as "Nature's contributions to people." These services extend beyond the tangible to encompass provisioning, supporting, regulating, and cultural aspects, making mangrove forests invaluable contributors to environmental health.


Provisioning Ecosystem Services

Provisioning ecosystem services are tangible benefits directly obtained from ecosystems. Mangrove forests serve as a source of timber and fuel wood, meeting the local demand for construction materials and energy. Additionally, these ecosystems play a vital role in providing drinking water to communities, filtering and purifying water as it passes through their intricate root systems.

Supporting Ecosystem Services

Supporting ecosystem services offered by mangrove forests are crucial for the maintenance of estuarine ecosystems. Acting as breeding and nursery habitats for various fish species, mangroves support the rich biodiversity of intertidal areas. This breeding ground serves as a crucial environment for marine creatures along coastlines, contributing significantly to the abundance and diversity of marine life.

The role of mangroves in preventing coastal erosion is pivotal. The intricate network of roots not only breaks down plant matter, reducing material decomposition and increasing carbon storage but also enriches the soil with organic matter from living roots, dead leaves, and woody materials. This, in turn, helps armor the soil against erosive forces, trapping and binding soil particles, thereby reducing erosion.

Furthermore, mangroves act as a natural shield against storms and floods. The thickets of mangrove trees buttress tidal mudflats, providing a buffer zone that protects the land from wind and wave damage. The canopy of mangrove forests reduces wind speeds, and the peat formed from organic matter absorbs water during heavy rains, lowering the risk of flooding.

Regulation Ecosystem Services

Regulation of estuaries is another critical function of mangrove ecosystems.

Their carbon sequestration capabilities exceed those of other forests, making them potent contributors to global warming mitigation by absorbing carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. The value of carbon stored in the plant matter, coupled with the breakage of plant matter buried in soil, further increases carbon storage.

Mangroves also influence the microclimate of their surroundings, creating a unique environment that sustains diverse flora and fauna.

Cultural Ecosystem Services

The presence of mangrove forest, contributes to cultural ecosystem services such as recreation, spiritual values, and biodiversity. The biodiversity of living organisms in the mangrove ecosystem is particularly important. The mangrove trees around the world contains around 73 species of trees and shrubs. The structure of the forest serves as a protective haven for the young of marine species to nest and reproduce. Moreover, the forests offers a resting site for shorebirds and migratory birds.

The Value of Ecosystem Services

Despite their immense value, the true worth of mangrove ecosystems often eludes traditional economic assessments. Environmental valuation studies, aimed at determining the value of ecosystem services, provide a comprehensive understanding of the goods and services that assist in making decisions about sustainable forest management. In 2018, the value of mangrove forests was estimated to be approximately 21,100 USD per hectare, yet this value does not fully capture the importance of these services to the residents who directly depend on them for their livelihoods and well-being.
